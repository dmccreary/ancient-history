    Let me verify the most current concept catalogs for this scope so the recommendations are solid.# Positioning the Course & Concept Sources

    Good news: the 1200 CE handoff you've already chosen is the most defensible break point in modern world-history pedagogy. The College Board redesigned AP World History: Modern to begin at exactly 1200 CE, which means there's a recognized scholarly consensus that you can cite directly. Your two courses will mirror the AP split, which is a credibility argument that registrars and articulation officers immediately recognize.

    The Concept Catalog That Maps Almost Perfectly to Your Scope

    The single most useful match is World History for Us All (whfua.history.ucla.edu), a free model curriculum from UCLA's National Center for History in the Schools and San Diego State. It's organized into nine "Big Eras," and the first five cover exactly your timeframe:

    • Big Era 1: Humans in the Universe (13 billion – 200,000 years ago)
    • Big Era 2: Human Beings Almost Everywhere (200,000 – 10,000 BP)
    • Big Era 3: Farming and the Emergence of Complex Societies (10,000 – 1000 BCE)
    • Big Era 4: Expanding Networks of Exchange and Encounter (1200 BCE – 500 CE)
    • Big Era 5 (partial): Patterns of Interregional Unity (300 – 1500 CE) — use only the 300–1200 CE portion

    Why this is high-credibility: The curriculum is a collaboration of San Diego State University and UCLA's National Center for History in the Schools, organizing the human past into nine Big Eras encompassing changes around the globe, and the site is directly associated with scholarship promoted by both the World History Association and the International Big History Association. Each Big Era has published "Landscape Teaching Units" and "Closeup Teaching Units" with explicit concept inventories, primary-source sets, and learning objectives you can cite by unit number.

    The framework also gives you a built-in conceptual spine: three themes — Humans and the Environment, Humans and Other Humans, and Humans and Ideas. This is convenient for concept-graph work because every topic in the course can be tagged against (a) its Big Era and (b) its theme, giving you a 2D taxonomy out of the box.

    Other Reputable Concept Sources for Pre-1200 CE

    These give you cross-validation if any one source is questioned:

    • National Standards for World History (UCLA/NCHS) — Eras 1–4 plus part of Era 5 cover your scope. The standards are explicit about content concepts and historical-thinking skills at each era.
    • AP World History: Modern course and exam description — even though the exam starts at 1200 CE, the official framework explicitly references the pre-1200 foundations students are expected to bring in. Citing "concepts foundational to AP World History" anchors you to College Board language.
    • OpenStax World History, Volume 1: to 1500 — peer-reviewed OER with a published learning-objective list per chapter; widely adopted at accredited institutions, so its concept inventory is itself a credibility chip.
    • Big History Project / OER Project — In 2020, Gates Ventures, the company founded by Bill Gates that developed the online open education resource course on Big History, launched the World History Project as a companion. The OER Project's "World History Origins to the Future" course covers exactly your window and publishes its conceptual scope-and-sequence openly.
    • Cambridge World History, Volumes I–V (CUP, 2015) — the academic-press counterpart. Vol. I (Beginnings), Vol. II (Early Agricultural Societies), Vol. III (Early Cities), Vol. IV (1500 BCE – 500 CE), Vol. V (500 CE – 1500 CE). Citing volume editors is the most academically defensible way to reference concepts.

    How to Avoid Duplication: The Theme-Level Boundary

    The cleanest non-overlap rule is to keep your course on themes that conclude or transform around 1200 CE, and let your existing course pick up themes that emerge or accelerate after 1200 CE:

    Belongs in your pre-1200 course Belongs in your existing 1200+ course
    Out-of-Africa, peopling of continents Mongol Eurasian integration
    Neolithic transition, domestication Black Death and demographic restructuring
    Independent emergence of primary civilizations Renaissance, Reformation
    Bronze Age systems and Late Bronze Age collapse Trans-oceanic European voyages
    Axial Age (~800–200 BCE) and the rise of universalizing thought Gunpowder empires
    Classical empires (Han, Rome, Maurya/Gupta, Achaemenid, Parthian, Sasanian) Atlantic system, plantation slavery
    Formation of universal religions (Buddhism, Christianity, Islam, classical Hinduism) Scientific Revolution, Enlightenment
    Late antiquity transformations Industrial Revolution
    First wave of Silk Road, Indian Ocean, trans-Saharan networks (pre-Mongol) Modern nation-state, modern empires
    Post-classical polities: Tang/early Song, Carolingians, Byzantium, classic Maya, Heian Japan, early Ghana World Wars, decolonization, globalization

    A "Bridge" Unit to Make the Handoff Explicit

    End the course with a final unit titled something like "The World on the Eve of Integration, c. 1100–1200 CE" that previews — without teaching — the conditions your second course will pick up: Song economic revolution, the maturity of dar al-Islam as a trade and scholarly network, European agricultural revolution and urban revival, expanding maritime trade in the Indian Ocean. This serves three purposes for credibility: it shows reviewers an explicit articulation between your courses, it gives students conceptual continuity, and it lets you cite the AP World History: Modern starting context as your handoff target.

    Practical Suggestion for Your Concept Graph

    Given your existing learning-graph infrastructure, I'd suggest tagging every concept node with three attributes:

    1. Big Era (1–5, from World History for Us All) — temporal location
    2. Theme (Environment / Other Humans / Ideas) — conceptual axis
    3. AHA Tuning competency (1–6) — skill axis

    That gives you a 3D tag space that's fully traceable to peer-reviewed sources, and lets you generate "where does this concept live in the established curriculum" justification automatically for any node. It also makes the boundary with your 1200+ course mechanical: anything tagged Big Era ≥ 6 is out of scope.
